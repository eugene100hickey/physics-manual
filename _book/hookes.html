3  Hooke’s Law Extension of a Spring

Every spring has a stiffness called its Spring Constant, \(k\), measured in Newtons per metre. Stiff, rigid springs have high values of \(k\), loose, floppy springs have low values of \(k\). We’re going to measure \(k\) by applying weights to a spring and seeing by how much it gets longer.

3.1 Experimental Set-Up

The apparatus will be set-up something like the sketch on the left. This is the first point; the weight holder and two 100g weights giving a total load of 0.300 kg.

Measure the position of the bottom of the weight holder on the vertical metre stick. This is the Position value for the table. The Extension value will be 0.000m

Now add an extra 100g weight and measure the new position. The Extension value wil now be the abolute difference between this position and the position before we added the extra mass.

  • the values in the table are for Extra Mass Added on top of the original 0.300kg. So the weight holder plus \(6 \times 100g\) would be an extra mass added of \(0.700kg - 0.300kg = 0.400kg\).

  • the extension values are always positive, it’s the absolute difference that matters.

  • for extension, it is always the difference between position and the very first position measurement. So the extension values should always be getting bigger as you descend the table.

  • When filling out your table, pay special attention to decimal places. The number of decimal places for \(Position\) and \(Extension\) should be \(3\), we measure to the nearest mm.

3.3 Calculation of the Spring Constant, k

Hooke’s Law states that the spring extension is directly proportional to the force making the spring stretch (in this case the weights, the masses times gravity)

\(F \; = \; k \times (extension)\) where \(k\) is the spring constant.

\(\implies \; W \; = mg \; = \; k \times (extension)\)

\(\implies \; m \; = \; \frac{k}{g} \times (extension)\)

so the slope of our graph will be \(\frac{k}{g}\), or \(k \; = \; g \times slope\).

We use this last equation to calculate \(k\) in N/m.

3.4 Discussion

There are four parts to the discussion section:

  • the main results - repeat the value of k obtained from the end of your calculations. Even though it is written elsewhere in your report, it’s important to repeat it here

  • the text book (manufacturers) value - the springs used have a rated k value of 100 N/m

  • inaccuracies - your value for \(k\) won’t be exactly the same as the manufacturers, nor will your graph be a perfect straight line. We need to try and account for these discrepancies. Pick one feature of the experiment and investigate whether it is an issue in the accuracy of your results. For example, are the weights all exactly 100g, does it matter if we start with a 0.300kg load and make it heavier, or start with a heavy load and decrease it….. You’ll need to examine the results you have already as well as gathering additional evidence by taking further measurements. Your idea might well be a key issue in the quality of the results we obtain, or it might not be and you are thus ruling it out. Both are valid outcomes of this error analysis.

  • improvements - based on the inaccuracy section above, can you suggest a way in which we could make our experiment better?

Retort stand, weight holder, 9 \(\times\) 100g weights, metre stick, 100 N/m spring. Make sure that the bottom of the weight holder is just touching the scale on the metre stick so measuring it’s position can be done accurately.
